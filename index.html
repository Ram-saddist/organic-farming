How to start organic farming in India

Organic farming, a sustainable approach to agriculture, has gained significant traction in India in recent years. It champions the use of natural techniques such as crop rotation, composting, and biological pest control while eschewing synthetic fertilizers and pesticides. The surge in popularity can be attributed to several factors.

Firstly, the growing awareness of the health benefits of organic food has led consumers to perceive it as a safer and healthier alternative, given concerns about synthetic chemicals in conventional farming. Additionally, mounting worries about environmental degradation caused by conventional agriculture, including water pollution, soil erosion, and biodiversity loss, have fuelled the appeal of organic farming as an eco-friendly choice.

Furthermore, the Indian government's support through initiatives like the National Programme for Organic Production has played a pivotal role in promoting organic farming.

The relevance of organic farming in India is multifaceted. India is a major agricultural producer and has the potential to become a global leader in organic farming. The country also boasts a rich tradition of sustainable agriculture, with many farmers already practicing organic methods. In the face of challenges like water scarcity, soil degradation, and climate change, organic farming can offer sustainable solutions.

To further promote organic farming in India, several actions can be taken. These include raising awareness among consumers about the benefits of organic food and the significance of supporting organic farmers. Financial support, especially for transitioning to organic methods and obtaining certification, is essential to alleviate the financial burdens faced by organic farmers. Developing a robust supply chain is crucial, and collaboration between the government and the private sector can facilitate this. Moreover, investment in research and development for new organic farming practices and improving crop productivity and quality can bolster the organic farming sector.

In conclusion, India has the potential to become a global leader in organic farming by embracing sustainable practices. The country's rich agricultural heritage, combined with increasing awareness and government support, positions it favourably in the growing organic farming movement. By taking these steps and fostering a holistic approach, India can reap the numerous benefits that organic farming has to offer, including improved food safety, environmental conservation, and agricultural sustainability.

Starting your organic farming journey in India involves several crucial steps to ensure success. Here's a comprehensive guide on how to get started:

1. Certification: Obtaining organic certification is mandatory if you want to get govt’s seal of approval. It opens doors to premium markets and fosters consumer trust. India has several accredited organic certification agencies, which you can find on the Agricultural and Processed Food Products Export Development Authority (APEDA) website.

2. Select a Suitable Site: Choose a location with healthy soil and no prior chemical contamination. Consider a soil test to evaluate the soil quality.

3. Prepare the Soil: Good soil quality is the foundation of organic farming. Enhance it by adding compost, manure, and other organic matter. Depending on your soil test results, you might need to incorporate lime or phosphorus.

4. Choose Crops and Livestock: Opt for crops and livestock that thrive in your region's climate. Prioritize crop rotation and diversity to maintain soil health and reduce pests and diseases.

5. Purchase Organic Inputs: Organic seeds, fertilizers, and pesticides are essential. India has a growing network of organic suppliers. You can also produce your own organic inputs, like compost and manure.

6. Implement Organic Practices: Embrace organic farming techniques such as crop rotation, cover cropping, composting, and biological pest control. The APEDA website and other organic farming resources offer valuable information.

There is a process of getting your farm organic certified, the officials from APEDA vist every three years to check the soil and organic farming practices.

Tips for Effective Soil Preparation:

Soil Testing: Assess pH, nutrient levels, and other soil characteristics.

Add Organic Matter: Mix in compost, manure, or green manure.

Till the Soil: Enhance drainage by tilling in organic matter.

Create Raised Beds: Improve drainage and reduce soil compaction.

Mulch the Soil: Suppress weeds and retain moisture.

Managing Nutrients in Organic Farming:

Crop Rotation and Cover Cropping: Maintain soil fertility.

Regularly Add Compost and Manure: Enhance soil health.

Use Organic Fertilizers: Options like bone meal, fish meal, and seaweed is beneficial. Avoid synthetic fertilizers.

Integrated Weed Management:

Preventive Measures: Reduce weeds with crop rotation, cover cropping, and mulching.

Hand-Weeding: Regularly remove weeds by hand.

Use Organic Herbicides: Consider options like corn gluten meal and vinegar. Steer clear of synthetic herbicides.

Pest and Disease Management:

Preventive Measures: Lower pest and disease pressure with strategies like crop rotation and good sanitation.

Biological Pest Control: Implement beneficial insects and microbials.

Use Organic Pesticides: Consider neem oil and insecticidal soap. Avoid synthetic pesticides.

Organic farming has gained significant popularity worldwide, including in India, due to its environmentally friendly and sustainable practices. Here are some key characteristics, advantages, and profitability aspects of organic farming, particularly in India:

Key Characteristics of Organic Farming:

· No Synthetic Chemicals: Organic farming avoids the use of synthetic pesticides, herbicides, and fertilizers. Instead, it relies on natural and organic alternatives.

· Soil Health: Organic farmers focus on maintaining and improving soil health through composting, crop rotation, and reduced soil disturbance.

· Biodiversity: Encouraging diversity of crops and using natural pest control methods promote a healthier ecosystem.

· Sustainable Practices: Organic farming aims to be sustainable in the long term, minimizing environmental impact and conserving resources.

Advantages of Organic Farming:

· Environmental Benefits: Organic farming reduces soil and water pollution, conserves biodiversity, and minimizes the carbon footprint.

· Healthier Produce: Organic products are typically free from synthetic chemicals, making them safer for consumers.

· Soil Enrichment: Organic practices enhance soil fertility and structure, leading to higher-quality crops.

· Premium Prices: Organic produce often commands higher prices in the market due to its perceived health and environmental benefits.

Certainly, in India, there's a diverse range of organic manures that play a crucial role in nurturing the soil and promoting sustainable agriculture. Let's explore some of these organic fertilizers:

Farmyard Manure (FYM): This is probably the most widespread organic manure in India. FYM is a blend of cattle and livestock dung, urine, along with straw and bedding materials. It's rich in essential nutrients that plants crave, and it also enhances soil structure and water retention capacity.

Green Manure: Green manure is all about growing specific crops to be mixed into the soil for fertility enhancement. Typically, legumes like cowpea, mung bean, and soybean are used. These legumes can fix atmospheric nitrogen in the soil, a vital nutrient for plant growth.

Compost: Compost is created by breaking down organic materials like crop residues, kitchen scraps, and garden waste. It's a nutrient-rich source and contributes to better soil structure and water retention.

Vermicompost: This exceptional fertilizer is produced by decomposing organic materials with the help of earthworms. Vermicompost is not only highly nutritious but also teeming with beneficial microorganisms, making it a fantastic choice for organic farming.

Biofertilizers: Biofertilizers introduce live microorganisms into the soil to enhance fertility and plant growth. These include nitrogen-fixing bacteria, phosphate-solubilizing bacteria, and mycorrhizal fungi.

While organic farming offers numerous benefits, it's not without its challenges. Here are some constraints that farmers often face when transitioning to organic practices:

Higher Input Costs: Organic inputs like fertilizers and pesticides can be pricier compared to conventional options.

Lower Yields: In some cases, especially during the initial years of conversion, organic yields may be lower than conventional ones.

Lack of Awareness: Many farmers may not fully grasp the advantages of organic farming, or the practices involved.

Limited Market Access: India has a relatively small market for organic products, making it challenging for farmers to sell their produce at fair prices.

The Indian government is actively addressing these constraints through various schemes and initiatives. They provide subsidies for organic inputs and certification and are working towards promoting organic products in the market. Despite the challenges, organic farming is gaining momentum in India. Farmers are increasingly recognizing the benefits it brings to their soil, crops, and overall health.

The Indian government is really committed to boosting organic farming, and they've rolled out several schemes to support farmers in making the transition to organic practices. Here are some of the key initiatives:

Paramparagat Krishi Vikas Yojana (PKVY): This is the flagship scheme, providing financial assistance for cluster-based organic farming, Participatory Guarantee System (PGS) certification, training, and marketing.

Mission Organic Value Chain Development for Northeastern Region (MOVCDNER): Specifically targeting the North Eastern Region, it offers financial support for cluster-based organic farming, PGS certification, training, marketing, and infrastructure development.

Capital Investment Subsidy Scheme (CISS) under Soil Health Management Scheme: It assists farmers in purchasing organic inputs like compost machines, vermicompost units, and biofertilizers.

National Mission on Oilseeds and Oil Palm (NMOOP): This mission provides financial assistance to farmers for cultivating oilseeds and oil palm using organic practices.

National Food Security Mission (NFSM): Provides financial assistance for cultivating food crops using organic practices.

Besides these, the government extends support for organic farming through the Rashtriya Krishi Vikas Yojana (RKVY) and various state and central government schemes.

Beyond government initiatives, several NGOs, private companies, and other organizations are also actively promoting organic farming. Some of these include:

Participatory Guarantee System (PGS): A community-based certification system for organic products where farmers, consumers, and stakeholders collaborate in the certification process.

Organic Farming Information and Advisory Centre (OFIAC): A national-level center providing information and advisory services on organic farming.

National Centre of Organic Farming (NCOF): This research and development institute focuses on organic farming technologies and practices.

If you're looking for financial assistance to kickstart your organic farming venture, several banks in India offer specific loan schemes:

NABARD Organic Farming Loan Scheme

SBI Organic Farming Loan Scheme

HDFC Bank Organic Farming Loan Scheme

These schemes typically cover expenses for purchasing organic inputs, transitioning to organic farming, and setting up organic processing units.

The investment required for an organic farming business can vary widely based on factors like farm size, crop type, and automation level. Small organic farms might need an investment as low as Rs. 20,000-30,000 per acre, while larger operations could require over Rs. 1 lakh per acre. You can lower these costs by adopting cost-effective practices such as composting, vermicomposting, and using biofertilizers.

In conclusion, the Indian government and various organizations are providing substantial support for organic farming. There's a range of government schemes, bank loans, and resources available to aid farmers in transitioning to organic practices. The investment needed depends on several factors but can be relatively affordable for smaller farms.
